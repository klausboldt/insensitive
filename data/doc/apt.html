APT

The Attached Proton Test is a simple method for 13C NMR that combines signal enhancement by decoupling with the ability to determine the number of protons that are attached to each carbon atom.

At the beginning the experiment a proton decoupling sequence is applied to the spin system, which transfers magnetisation from the more sensitive I spins to the insensitive S spins via nuclear Overhauser enhancement.

The pulse sequence begins with a 90° pulse from x-direction on the S spins, which creates transverse magnetisation. The proton decoupling is then shut off, and the spins are allowed to evolve for a period that is approximately the inverse of the CH 1J-coupling constant (c.a. 125 Hz or 8 ms). It is followed by a 180° pulse from x and another delay of J-1, while proton decoupling is turned on. This creates a spin echo and refocusses the magnetisation.
Depending on the number of couplings, the magnetisation will be oriented either along -Iy (CH3, CH) or Iy (CH2, C). This results in primary and tertiary carbon atoms giving positive signals, while secondary and quaternary carbon atoms give rise to negative signals.

The APT sequence that is used in routine experiments includes a first pulse with an optimal Ernst angle < 90°, which allows for a higher repetition rate of the experiment. Since the 180° pulse then inverts the magnetisation to have a component along -Iz, another 180° pulse must be applied to guarantee fast relaxation.

The APT experiment is slightly less sensitive than the similar DEPT sequence, but is able to show all possible carbon configurations in one experiment.
