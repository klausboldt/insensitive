Trajectory of Single Spins

For spin, like all quantum mechanical angular momentum, the Uncertainly Principle dictates that only the absolute square s2 of the angular momentum and one of the cartesian components sz can be known at the same time, while the other components sx and sy are completely unknown. This has led to the common depiction of spin as "double cones" that point either upwards or downwards along the principal axis (z in this example), on the surface of which the orthogonal spin components are smeared out and precess. Drawing single spins as classical vectors is, hence, not possible.

The density matrix description of NMR and all its derived models in Insensitive like the vector model are based on statistical quantum mechanics. It averages over a spin ensemble and yields the expectation value for the magnetisation that is obtained after the measurement of a large number spins. This limits the visualisation capabilities of the program in the case of dephasing, or simply for understanding how the behaviour of individual spins leads to the macroscopic result of the NMR experiment. Therefore, the trajectories of individual spins is shown in the "Single Spins" module.

The uncertainty of the cartesian components of spin in an external magnetic field is expressed through a cyclic commutation relation

In an external magnetic field B0, if the z component is known through a measurement to be either up or down (α or β state), the x and y components are completely undetermined and the vector is smeared out over the whole cone. If, instead, the x component is known, the cyclic commutation rule under the influence of the B0 in z direction converts the certainty of the x component into certainty along the -y direction, then into -x and y in a cyclic manner. Physically, this is equal to precession around the z axis.

A linear polarised rf-pulse with strength 2B1 cos(ωt) perturbs the B0 field. Linear polarised radiation can be split in two circular polarised beams with opposite direction. Of these beams, one rotates opposite to the precession of the spins and, hence, can be neglected for being off-resonance. The other has a magnetic field component B1 that varies periodically from x to -y to -x to y back to x, following the polarisation vector. That tilts the principal axis of the system is slightly away from z to align with the periodically changing total magentic field B0 + B1. (The tilt angle, as well as the relative precession frequencies around the z and x axes, are exaggerated for didactic reasons.) This time-dependent perturbation puts the system in a perpetual non-equilibrium state in which the spins never relax into a new equilibrium and the uncetainty shifts between the cartesian components. The oscillating tilt of the magnetic field leads to a movement of the total angular momentum of the spin ensemble to follow a spiralling path, called nutation.

Viewed from the perspective of the rotating frame, the resonant spins no longer precess around the z axis, because the effect of the dominating field along z is mathematically removed. However, despite the apparent disappearance of the B0 field, the non-equilibrium persists, yielding a continuous rotating of the spin states around the x axis, which turns z magnetisation into -y into -z into y and back into z magnetisation. Once the rf-field is switched off the spins that are oriented in y direction are in a superposition state of both α and β with respect to the static B0 field.

With no pulse acting on the spins, relaxation returns the spins into the state of thermal equilibrium. Longitudinal (T1) relaxation flips spins from the β to the α state until the ratio between the occupied states follows the Boltzmann distribution. Transversal (T2) relaxation causes dephasing and loss of coherence of the individual spins around the z axis. (Note that the difference between the occupation of the α and β state is strongly exaggerated. In reality, the difference in occupation probability is 8 x 10-5 for protons at 300 K, using a 500 MHz spectrometer.)

The single spin precession is simulated through uncoupled spins, which are exactly on resonance with the rf pulse, i.e. Ω0 = 0 Hz. Insensitive can simulate three different conditions: in the presence of a static B0 field, during the application of the B1 field of the rf pulse, and in the absence of any field. In addition, the spin states can be shown in the static or rotating frame.

Insensitive either calculates the uncertainty of a single spin and depicts it as colour shading of the cones if the slider for the number of spins is moves to the right, or simulates between 1 and 1000 individual spins. In the absence of any magnetic field Insensitive displays the spins as vectors pointing from the origin. It is important to note that these states cannot be observed directly in an experiment and represent a hypothetical snapshot of the spin ensemble. For a measurement an external magnatic field needs to be applied, which determines the principal axis, usually denoted with z for the B0 field. The measurement determines one of the cartesian components of the angular momentum. The uncertainty of the other components is shown as fluctuations of the individual vectors and results in a random distribution in the x and y directions.

The Single Spin module of Insensitive needs to be treated as an illustration of the breakdown of the vector model for spin rather than a depiction of reality.
