Trajectory of Single Spins

The density matrix description of NMR is limited to describe the behaviour of a spin ensemble and yields the expectation value for the magnetisation that is obtained after the measurement of a large number spins. This limits the visualisation capabilities of the program in the case of dephasing, or simply for understanding how the behaviour of individual spins leads to the macroscopic result of the NMR experiment. Therefore, the trajectories of individuals spins is shown in the "Single Spins" module.

The most important principle to describe single spins under the influence of external magentic fields is that only one cartesian component of angular momentum can be measured with certainty: if the z component is known through a measurement to be either up or down (α or β state), the x and y components are completely undetermined. The relationship between the three cartesian components is expressed through a cyclic commutation rule:

As one of two components becomes more determined (e.g. Ix) it converts the certainty of the second component (e.g. Iz) into certainty of the third one (Iy). This is equal to a rotation around (in this example) the x axis as a result of the rf-pulse from x-direction.

Physically, the presence of a rotating B1 field perpendicular to the principal z axis tilts the axis of the total magentic field B0 + B1 slightly in a time-dependent manner. (The tilt angle, as well as the relative precession frequencies around the z and x axes, are exaggerated for didactic reasons.) This time-dependent perturbation puts the system in a perpetual non-equilibrium state. The small B1 field of the rf-pulse in x direction converts z magnetisation into -y magnetisation. However, the rotation of the circularly polarised radiofrequency never allows the spins to relax into the new equilibrium. The oscillating tilt of the magnetic field leads to a movement of the total angular momentum of the spin ensemble to follow a spiralling path, called nutation.

Viewed from the perspective of the rotating frame, the resonant spins no longer precess around the z axis. However, despite the apparent disappearance of the B0 field, the non-equilibrium persists, yielding a continuous rotating of the spin states around the x axis, turning z magnetisation into -y into -z into y and back into z magnetisation. Once the rf-field is switched off the spins that are oriented in y direction are in a superposition state of both α and β with respect to the static B0 field.

With no pulse acting on the spins, relaxation returns the spins into the state of thermal equilibrium. Longitudinal (T1) relaxation flips spins from the β to the α state until ratio between the occupied states follows the Boltzmann distribution. Transversal (T2) relaxation causes dephasing and loss of coherence of the individual spins around the z axis. (Note that the difference between the occupation of the α and β state is strongly exaggerated. In reality, the difference in occupation probability is 8 x 10-5 for protons at 300 K, using a 500 MHz spectrometer.)

The single spin precession is simulated through uncoupled spins, which are exactly on resonance with the rf pulse, i.e. Ω0 = 0 Hz. Insensitive can simulate three different conditions: in the presence of a static B0 field, during the application of the B1 field of the rf pulse, and in the absence of any field. In addition, the spin states can be shown in the static or rotating frame. In the absence of any magnetic field Insensitive displays the spin states of up to 100 random spins as vectors starting at the origin. These states cannot be observed directly in an experiment. For a measurement an external magnatic field needs to be applied, which determines the principal axis, which is usually the z axis for B0. The measurement determines one of the cartesian components of the angular momentum. Hence, the other two components are completely uncertain. This results in a random distribution of the vectors in the x and y directions, and one of two possible orientations in z direction, leading to a double cone. The indeterminancy of the x and y components are visualised through the large number of vectors. In reality, if the state of a spin is known, either α pointing in +z direction or β pointing in -z direction, its x or y component cannot actually be drawn. A visualisation that would describe the state better would be a cone that is completely shaded in the colour used for the vector.
