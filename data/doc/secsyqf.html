SECSY

The spin echo correlation spectroscopy (SECSY) is a correlation technique similar to the COSY. It was designed to decrease the spectrum size in the days when storage was precious and is interesting for historical reasons only. In the pulse sequence a second 90° pulse is centered in the middle of the variable evolution time t1.

The SECSY spectrum can be derived from the 2D J-resolved spectrum, which employs a flip angle of 180° instead of a 90° for the pulse during the indirect evolution time. This refocuses the chemical shift during t1, so that only half the coupling constant ±1/2 J survives as the apparent precession frequency. The peaks are therefore split by the coupling constant along the F1 domain.

With a 90° angle for the second pulse the magnetisation components of both coupled spins are mixed, leading to four possible apparent precession frequencies during t1: 0 Hz, ±1/2 J, ±1/2 ΔΩ, and ±1/2 (ΔΩ ± J).

The evolution during t2 is analogous to the COSY experiment. Both spectra display the same information, but in SECSY the autocorrelated peaks appear along the centre of the F1 axis, as in a J-resolved spectrum. The cross peaks appear at the difference of the chemical shift values of the coupling spins.

Post acquisition the spectrum can be skewed to transform the horizontal peaks into diagonal shape.

This creates the same geometry as the COSY spectrum of the same spin system.

Due to the compression along the F1 axis the data can be acquired with two thirds the spectral width of a full COSY spectrum while retaining the same information. Today digital storage is not a concern and the SECSY experiment is no longer in use.
