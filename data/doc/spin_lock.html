Spin lock

For a spin-lock a sequence of closely spaced 180°-pulses is applied to a spin system. Any coherences that are oriented along the axis of the pulses will be constantly refocussed and have no chance to dephase. This effectively suppresses T2-relaxation and chemical shifts.

In a spin locked system the transverse relaxation can cause cross relaxation via dipole-dipole coupling, a mechanism that is used for coherence transfer in the ROESY experiment. Also, due to being held along one axis of the rotating frame all spin-locked spins are magnetically equivalent and strongly coupled. This is put to use in the TOCSY experiment.

An important use of spin-locking especially for solid state NMR methods is cross polarisation (CP). It requires two different spins I and S to have the same Larmor frequency. This so-called Hartmann-Hahn condition can be achieved by adjusting the power of the different rf-channels so that the spin-lock pulses take equal time. If this requirement is fulfilled, magnetisation will flow from cold to hot spins. Hot spins are those spins which are weakly oriented and have a small magnetic moment, cold spins are highly oriented and have a large magnetic moment.
