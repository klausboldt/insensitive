Shaped Pulses

A radiofrequency pulse in NMR is normally assumed to be of approximately rectangular shape, i.e. the B1 field with frequency Ω0 is switched on with a constant power γB1 for a specific time t and then turned off again. Such a field that has limited duration has a finite frequency spectrum based on Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle. The spectrum can be calculated from the pulse shape using Fourier transformation. A rectangular function transforms into sinc(x) = sin(x)/x, which implies that the field experienced by spins with a Larmor frequency with an offset from Ω0 undergoes frequency-dependent oscillations.

The excitation profile does not match the Fourier transformation of the pulse shape exactly, because one has to consider off-resonance effects: if the frequency of the rf-field does not match the Larmor frequency of a spin exactly the spin will experience both the B1 and B0 field with a ratio defined by the resonance offset. Aside from a smaller flip angle than for an on-resonance spin the magnetisation will also acquire a phase shift. In the Pulse Shape module this excitation profile of a pulse can be plotted as either absolute Mxy magnetisation and phase shift, as Mx and My components, and as the residual Mz magnetisation.

For full excitation of a whole spectrum short, hard, rectangular pulses can cover the complete spectral range. Off-resonance effects are more severe for nuclei with a wider range of chemical shifts (e.g. 13C compared to 1H). This is reflected in slightly smaller signal intensities far away from the spectrum centre. The phase shift for large frequency offsets can be easily corrected using first order phase correction. However, for selective, soft pulses the off-resonance oscillations of the excitation profile poses a problem due to unwanted, weak excitation of the whole spectrum. This can be circumvented by shaped pulses.

Gauss pulses

The Fourier transform of a Gauss function is again a Gauss function. The width in the frequency domain is reciprocal to the one in time domain, which implies that with a long Gauss pulse a limited range of the spectrum can be excited, while no unwanted excitation occurs outside that range. The disadvantage is that the excitation profile of a Gauss pulse causes excitations with different intensities and strong phase shifts across the width of the Gauss profile.

Sinc pulses

Reciprocity of the Fourier transformation implies that the excitation profile of a sinc(x) pulse shape will be a rectangular function. This allows for selective excitation of a limited range of the spectrum with near constant intensity. However, the spins still experience a strong frequency-dependent phase shift.

BURP pulses

Approximately rectangular excitation profiles with small phase shifts have been achieved with the so-called BURP pulses. These have complex shapes composed of several sine and cosine functions and complex trajectories of the magnetisation vectors. They have been conceived by reverse engineering the desired excitation profile. Several versions exist for 90° excitation (E-BURP, U-BURP) and 180° inversion (I-BURP, RE-BURP) pulses.

DANTE pulse train

Very selective pulses have been realised using the DANTE sequence (Delay Alternating Nutation Tailored Excitation). It is composed of equally spaced, short pulses that together cause on-resonance spins to flip for the desired angle. In the periods between the short pulses off-resonance spins will precess around Iz and effects of the pulse is averaged out. For a narrower excitation profile a larger number of shorter pulses is required. Additionally to the on-resonance excitation additional regions of the spectrum get excited, which appear at distances of 1/Δt from the resonance frequency, with Δt being the period between two pulses. This can also be understood by looking at the Fourier transformation of a comb function (equally spaced δ-functions), which is again a comb function.
