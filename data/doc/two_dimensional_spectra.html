2D Spectra

Two-dimensional spectra are acquired by repeating the pulse sequence of the experiment and recording the FID, while an evolution time t1 after the initial preparation period is systematically varied.

t1 is called the indirect time domain and introduces a phase shift between the consecutive FIDs. A mixing period follows, in which coherence transfer between spins takes place. To process the data, the Fourier transformation of each of the FIDs must be calculated. This results in a row of spectra in the direct frequency domain F2 with a systematic variation of the phase, as mentioned above. The signal intensity becomes weaker with longer t1 times.

A Fourier transformation of the signals with equal Larmor frequency as a function of t1 results in a spectrum in the indirect frequency domain F1. For computational reasons the resolution of the F1-domain is usually lower than for the directly observed F2-domain.

The frequency at which the peak appears in the F2-domain depends on the spin's Larmor frequency in the t2-domain, while the frequency in the F1-domain depends on the frequency of the magnetisation during the delay t1.

There exist two classes of two-dimensional spectra. In correlated spectra like the COSY-experiment the spectrum shows cross peaks between correlated spins, if the magnetisation evolves with the Larmor frequency of one spin during t1 and with the Larmor frequency of a second spin after the mixing period during t2. Contrary to this, resolved spectra display two independent aspects of a spectrum along the two dimensions. J-Resolved spectra for example separate the spin coupling from their chemical shifts and facilitate resolving overlapping multiplets.

The Fourier transformation of the indirect time domain cannot discriminate between clockwise and counterclockwise precession as the direct time domain with quadrature detection. This causes the 2D spectrum to be mirrored at the centre frequency. The mirrored signals can be removed by phase cycling, pulsed field gradients or taking amplitude modulated spectra.
