Vector model

Spin was first derived from the magnetic moment inherent to charged elementary particles, caused by an intrinsic rotational momentum. It is usually pictured as the normal vector perpendicular to the plane of rotation, following the right hand rule. To measure such a magnetic moment the system has to be brought into an external magnetic field B0, which as a convention defines the z-axis of the coordinate system. The gyromagnetic ratio γ is the proportionality factor between the magnetic moment and spin. As a result of quantum mechanics there exist two possible orientations of a spin 1/2 to the external field, α and β, with a slight energy difference. In thermal equilibrium that leads to a small excess of spins populating the α-state, following the Boltzman distribution.

It is this excess magnetisation that gives rise to the weak signal in the NMR experiment.
Also as a consequence from quantum mechanics the magnetic moments cannot align perfectly with the external field, which causes the normal vectors to precess around the B0 axis on the surface of a cone, either parallel or antiparallel to the z-axis. Its angular frequency is determined by the difference of the two energy levels.
The NMR experiment is always measured in a large ensemble of spins. Most of them cancel out, the excess magnetisation of spins distributed equally around the cone's surface gives rise to a magnetisation vector parallel to the +z-axis, while the orientation in x- or y-direction is random and averages to zero. This is the signal displayed in Insensitive. The Single Spins module allows to visualise the trajectory of individual spins.

Following from Heisenberg's uncertainty principle, if the orientation of a spin along one axis is known, the other two cartesian components become completely indeterminable. This happens for example by applying the field B0. This means that if a single spin's z-magnetisation is determined to be either +1/2 or -1/2 one will measure either one of these values for the x- or y-component with equal probability. A pulse of radio frequency irradiation can rotate the average magnetisation by providing another principal axis the spins precess around. A 90°x pulse from the x-direction rotates the spins around the axis of incidence for 90°, resulting in an average magnetisation along -y. After the pulse has ended and the field is again dominated by B0 the system has equally populated α and β levels, but a finite probability of any single spin vector rotating in phase with the others. This is called coherence. Only magnetisation in the x,y-plane can be detected.

Each kind of spin, depending on its chemial shift and the state of the spins it couples with, give rise to one vector in Insensitive's vector model. It can be chosen between three display modes, either as individual spins, as expectation values of magnetisation, where antiphase terms collapse to zero, and as the total average magnetisation seen by the detector.

Of all representations in this application the vector model has the largest restrictions, but it has its advantages because of the intuitive geometric interpretation of spin states (or magnetic moments) as vectors. The limits of the vector model are reached when coherence transfer takes place. This transfer of magnetisation between two coupled spins involves states that have no net magnetisation. They only describe the orientation of spins in relation to other spins in a coupled system and not to the external magnetic field. Still they are crucial in explaining most two-dimensional experiments like the H,H-COSY. Note that Insensitive only simulates the spin ensemble, never the single spins pictured above!
