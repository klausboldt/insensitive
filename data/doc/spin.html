Spin

'Spin' is a fundamental property of all matter. It results as a consequence of treating quantum mechanics relativistically, as was first done in Dirac's equation. The derivation assumes that spacetime is causal, continuous, uniform and isotropic. In special relativity, different frames of reference can be transformed into one another using the Lorentz transformation, which considers time t as an additional degree of freedom to the three spatial coordinates x, y and z. It can be shown using arguments from group theory that only properties s2 = x2 + y2 + z2 - c2t2 remain unchanged under Lorentz transformation. These are called Lorentz invariants and represent fundamental properties like the speed of light, rest mass, number of particles or electrical charge. There exists a further Lorentz invariant that takes a form similar to angular momentum, but involves the transformation between a spatial coordinate and time (i.e. a special Lorentz transformation or Lorentz boost) rather than a rotation between two spatial coordinates. This gives rise to what we refer to as spin.

As an inherently 4-dimensional object in spacetime, spin cannot be properly represented in 3-dimensional space. However, an opservable that arises from spin is a magnetic dipole moment μs, which historically has been attributed to an assumed rotational momentum S of a particle around its own axis (hence the name spin). The 3-dimensional vectors used to graphically represent spin in Insensitive are therefore representing the magnetic moment and not spin itself. In contrary to a normal rotational momentum like the orbital momentum of an electron, the spin's quantum number s is constant for a class of particles and may take half integer values (1/2 in case of an electron or a proton):

Not only is the spin's absolute value quantised, but its possible orientations as well. Quantum mechanics states that the spin vector's projection on any axis (e.g. z-direction) can only take values between s and -s in integer steps. These different states are degenerate in the absence of a magnetic field but split up when a magnetic field is applied. This is called Zeeman splitting and is proportional to the magnetic field. The spin projection is defined by the projection quantum number ms.

The cartesian components of any angular momentum do not commute. If one orientation ms is known, the other two components are completely uncertain.
After a magnetic field B0 (usually in z-direction) is applied, spins-1/2 will relax over time into one of two possible Zeeman states with ms = 1/2 (α) or ms = -1/2 (β) in a Boltzmann distribution.

In a magnetic field the orientation quantisation forces the spins in an angle to the magnetic field vector. The force towards the z-direction causes the rotational axis of the spins to precess around B0 with an angular frequency ω0 determined by the spin's gyromagnetic ratio γ and the magnetic field strength B0 called the Larmor frequency.

The operators for the three spin orientations Sx, Sy and Sz for a spin with s = 1/2 can be expressed by the three 2 x 2 Pauli matrices.

The diagonal elements represent the α and β states. The off-diagonal elements are coherences, superpositions of both states where the complex coefficients are in phase.

In the case of more complex nuclei the spin is understood as the total angular momentum I of all constituent protons and neutrons, which are in turn total angular moments of fundamental particles, the quarks. In NMR the nuclear spin is usually referring to the ground state. It is this property that is responsible for the NMR signal.
