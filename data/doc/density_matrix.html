Density matrix

In a large spin ensemble as in the NMR experiment the average spin state must be calculated using statistical quantum mechanics. This is done by expressing the expectation value for an operator Ω of the spin system ψ as a weighted sum with the statistical weight P(ψ) over the spin ensemble. ψ may be an eigenstate or a mixed state, a linear combination of eigenstates.

Thus the density matrix is a convenient way to calculate the ensemble average of any expectation value of ψ. One only needs to know the density matrix, which is easy for example in the case of the state of thermal equilibrium. Once known, any operation can be performed on the density matrix. The density matrix becomes increasingly complex with larger spin systems. For one spin the matrix is actually quite easy to read: The matrix diagonal elements are the relative populations (more precisely, the population probability densities) of the energy levels α (top left) and β (bottom right). This is equivalent to magnetisation in z- and -z-direction.

The off-diagonal elements are complex numbers, each pair of matrix elements mirrored at the diagonal being complex conjugates of each other, carrying the same information. They describe coherences between the two states (or in other words, x- and y-magentisation). The x,y-plane can be translated directly into the complex plane of the coherences, where x is the real and y the imaginary part.
