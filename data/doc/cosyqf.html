COSY

The simplest 2D NMR experiment is the COrrelation SpectroscopY that creates a two-dimensional, square spectrum where the one-dimensional proton spectrum appears in the diagonal. When two spins couple via J-coupling a cross peak will appear that connects the two coupling diagonal peaks. (Bruker code: cosyqf and cosyph)

The pulse sequence consists of two 90°-pulses separated by a variable time t1 that is being changed systematically. After the second 90°-pulse the FID is recorded over the time t2.

Set up a two-spin system of I-spins. Set the chemical shifts to Ω1 = 1.2 Hz for spin 1 and Ω2 = -1.2 Hz for spin 2. The coupling constant should be J12 = 0.5 Hz.

The first 90°x-pulse turns the operators I1z and I2z into transverse magnetisation. Over the evolution time t1 the in-phase magnetisation will evolve into antiphase magnetisation (see the page for the 1H-Spectrum for details). Just before the second pulse the product operator terms are:

- I1y cos(Ω1t1) cos(πJ12t1)
+ 2I1xI2z cos(Ω1t1) sin(πJ12t1)
+ I1x sin(Ω1t1) cos(πJ12t1)
+ 2I1yI2z sin(Ω1t1) sin(πJ12t1)

The second pulse will transfer magnetisation from spin 1 to spin 2 if the two spins are coupled via chemical bonds and antiphase terms exist at the time of the pulse. Just after the pulse the product operators read:

- I1z cos(Ω1t1) cos(πJ12t1)
- 2I1xI2y cos(Ω1t1) sin(πJ12t1)
+ I1x sin(Ω1t1) cos(πJ12t1)
- 2I1zI2y sin(Ω1t1) sin(πJ12t1)

The first term -I1y got turned into -I1z-magnetisation and is not observable. The third term I1x, being oriented along the rotation axis, is unaffected by the pulse and is thus in the observable state it was before and will account for the diagonal peaks in the spectrum. The product operator terms that come out of the in-phase terms are:

+ I1x sin(Ω1t1) cos(πJ12t1) cos(Ω1t2) cos(πJ12t2)
+ 2I1yI2z sin(Ω1t1) cos(πJ12t1) cos(Ω1t2) sin(πJ12t2)
+ I1y sin(Ω1t1) cos(πJ12t1) sin(Ω1t2) cos(πJ12t2)
- 2I1xI2z sin(Ω1t1) cos(πJ12t1) sin(Ω1t2) sin(πJ12t2)

The coherence transfer that accounts for the cross peaks is due to the antiphase terms. The pulse transforms the term 2I1yI2z into 2I1zI2y. Note that these terms evolve with the frequency Ω1 in t1 and with Ω2 in t2.

- 2I1zI2y sin(Ω1t1) sin(πJ12t1) cos(Ω2t2) cos(πJ12t2)
+ I2x sin(Ω1t1) sin(πJ12t1) cos(Ω2t2) sin(πJ12t2)
+ 2I1zI2x sin(Ω1t1) sin(πJ12t1) sin(Ω2t2) cos(πJ12t2)
+ I2y sin(Ω1t1) sin(πJ12t1) sin(Ω2t2) sin(πJ12t2)

The term 2I1xI2y after the second pulse is a non-observable multi quantum coherence and will not evolve into observable magnetisation again.

The figure shows the COSY spectrum of a 3 spin system with the Larmor frequencies Ω1 = 3.2 Hz, Ω2 = -2.7 Hz and Ω3 = 0.3 Hz. The coupling constants were set to J12 = 0.25 Hz and J23 = 0.37 Hz. The dwell time was set to 0.1 s and phase cycling was used.

If the FID starts with cosine-modulated in-phase signals and sine-modulated antiphase signals, the diagonal peaks will appear as broad dispersion signals after Fourier transformation and overlap with nearby cross peaks, which are in absorption mode. Often the mean square of the complex spectrum is used, which has pure absorption signals with a line width between that of the diagonal and cross peaks. The spectrum is recorded using phase cycling or pulse field gradients in order to remove signals that result from unwanted coherence pathways. In the case of COSY these stem from two sources: The magnetisation evolves both as +1 and -1 quantum coherences during t1, which causes the signals to appear mirrored at the carrier frequency along the F1 axis. In addition, single quantum coherences are created by the second pulse on z-magnetisation caused by relaxation during t1.

Step φ1 φ2 φrec
1 0° 0° 0°
2 90° 0° 270°
3 180° 0° 180°
4 270° 0° 90°

One possible four step phase cycle varies φ1 = {0°, 90°, 180°, 270°} and the receiver phase φrec = {0°, 270°, 180°, 90°} while keeping φ2 = 0° constant.

To destroy the +1 coherences (called P-type signals) and only retain signals from -1 coherences in the resulting N-type spectrum using gradients, two gradient pulses have to be applied before and after the second 90° pulse in the gCOSY experiment. To yield a P-type spectrum, the second gradient pulse has to be reversed.

All steps in the processing of the COSY spectrum can be simulated in Insensitive, including taking amplitude modulated spectra using the States or TPPI method.
Further 2D correlation experiments have been developed in order to yield narrower line shapes (DQF-COSY, z-COSY, E.COSY) or show correlations over more bonds (Relayed COSY, TOCSY).
