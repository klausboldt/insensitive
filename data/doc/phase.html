Phase factor

An eigenstate n of a quantum mechanical system is characterised by a complex coefficient cn = An exp(iφn), which is comprised of the amplitude An, which is a weighing factor for the population of the state, and a phase φn. The phase evolves with time, but for a pure eigenstate with An = 1 its value is largely inconsequential, because its value does not affect the eigenvalue for an operator of that eigenstate.

This changes for a coherent superposition state. "Superposition" means that the state is composed of contributions of several states in form of a linear combination c1Ψ1 + c2Ψ2 + ... so that the sum of the amplitudes are 1. For a spin 1/2 these are the spin up (α) and spin down (β) states. "Coherent" means that there is a defined phase relationship between the contributing states Ψn. This phase difference Δφ determines the orientation of the spin vector in the x,y-plane.

A phase shift of the rf-pulse determines which coherent superposition state is formed, starting from a pure excess population, or which coherence is able to interact with that pulse. In practical terms, pulse from the x direction (0°) will cause a rotation around the x-axis. A phase shift of 90° will cause the spin vectors to rotate around the y-axis.

If the phase of a spin is not zero (i.e., aligned along the -y axis following a pulse with a phase shift of 0°) at the beginning of the FID, the peaks in the spectrum will be phase shifted. This appears as a deviation of the pure absorption Lozentzian line shape of the real spectrum and a pure dispersion line shape of the imaginary spectrum to a mixture of absorption and dispersion line shapes. This can be corrected by multiplying the spectra with a phase factor. In zero order phase correction this is a constant complex number φ0 that shifts the phase of the whole spectrum, while in first order phase correction the factor φ1 depends linearly on the Larmor frequency and shifts the phase of the signals relatively to each other.
