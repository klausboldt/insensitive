Line shapes

The exponential decay of the FID causes the spectral lines to appear in the shape of a Lorentzian function after Fourier transformation, following the convolution theorem.

The resulting complex function has a real and an imaginary part, which need to be plotted separately.

The real part has the appearance of a typical spectral peak and is called an absorption line for historical reasons. (Sometimes the term emission line is found for a downwards pointing signal, but negative absorption line should be used to avoid confusion with actual emission processes.) The absorption line width (full width at half maximum) is determined by the parameter λ as w = 2λ.

The imaginary part features a zero passage at Ω0 and appears much broarder. This peak shape is called a dispersion line. It is responsible for a significant overlap of spectral lines in 2D spectra such as the COSY spectrum.

Mixed phase line shapes appear when the FID is acquired from a state where the spins are not in a pure Iy or Ix state.

The line shape can be changed to a Gauss peak by means of the Lorentz-Gauss transformation.

This line shape is narrower at the base than the Lorentzian and leads to better peak separation. A complete removal of dispersion signals for absolute value spectra is possible with the gaussian Pseudo-echo transformation.

The full width at half maximum is determined by the standard deviation σ.
