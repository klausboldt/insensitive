HMBC

The Heteronuclear Multi-Bond Correlation experiment records correlations between protons and heteronuclear spins with more than one bond in between. It works similar to the HMQC experiment. The number of bonds which can be seen in the spectrum is set by the fixed delay time τ = 1/(2JIS). The intensity of the correlation peaks are proportional to sin2(πJISτ). Since JIS for multi-bond coupling covers a much wider range than for single bonds the parameter needs to be adjusted for the correlations of interest.

The pulse sequence starts with a 90°x-pulse on the I-spins to create transverse megnetization, followed by a 90°x-pulse on the S-spins after a short delay that is set to match the single-bond coupling constants and converts these antiphase terms into multi-quantum coherences at a time when the long-range correlations have not yet significantly evolved. Another 90°x-pulse on the S-spins after the delay time τ then converts the antiphase terms of interest into multi-quantum coherences as in the HMQC experiment.

This is followed by the variable delay time t1 with a 180°x-pulse on the I-spins in the middle to form a spin echo sandwich. Only the chemical shifts evolve over this period. A last 90°x-pulse on the S-spins converts the double-quantum coherences back to antiphase magnetisation which is immediately detected. The HMBC experiment cannot be run with broadband decoupling and therefore shows J-couplings in the cross peaks. The magnetisation that results from the single-bond multi-quantum coherences needs to be removes by phase cycling.
