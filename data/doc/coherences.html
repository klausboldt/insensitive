Coherences

Coherences are superpositions of two or more eigenstates in a quantum mechanical system. They represent a state where two eigenstates are not only equally populated (this would lead to zero magnetisation) but also have the same complex phase factor. The complex plane of this phase factor can be viewed as the x,y-plane perpendicular to the direction of the external magnetic field. The complex coherence results in a transverse magnetisation.

The coherence order is the number of spins of synchronous phase. A population is independent from the spins' phase and is therefore of the order 0. Single-quantum coherences of the order 1 are an ensemble of spins, all with the same phase. Antiphase magnetisation also has the coherence order 1. Instead of being one set of spins with the same phase and finite magnetisation the antiphase terms represent coherences relative to another spin's population. Higher order coherences with the coherence order 2, 3, ... are states of two or more coupled spins in which the spins have a fixed phase relative to each other, but no net magnetisation in the ensemble average. In the case of 2I1xI2y the two spins have a fixed angle of 90° in the x,y-plane, but point either along x and y or -x and -y with the same probability.

In a density matrix coherences are represented by the off-diagonal matrix elements. Coherences can be positive or negative. The two possible coherences + and - are represented as complex conjugate matrix elements that are mirrored at the matrix diagonal. They can be geometrically interpreted as clockwise and counterclockwise rotations around Iz.

An easy way to get the coherence orders of a given spin state is to analyse the product operators in the spherical tensor basis. Changes in the coherence order of ±1 are mathematically described through the shift operators I+ and I-.
