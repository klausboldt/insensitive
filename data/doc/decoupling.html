Decoupling

If the observed nucleus has a low sensitivity, the coupling of the spins leads to a complicated multiplet structure or the coupling information is not needed (e.g. homonuclear coupling in heteronuclear correlation spectroscopy), it is advantageous to use decoupling. The coupling interactions to the spin that is decoupled is then suppressed, and the multiplet peaks collapse to single lines. The intensity of the signal is the combined intensity of the multiplet lines, which causes and a higher signal-to-noise ratio.

Decoupling can be achieved by irradiating one spin or spin type with the resonant frequency either continuously or with pulse sequences (MLEV-4, WALTZ-16, etc.). A rather simple pulse sequence is an array of closely spaced 180° pulses, which constantly invert the irradiated spins. This induces rapid transitions between the spin's energy levels on the NMR timescale. For coupling partners the spin appears to be in a time-averaged state and the coupling cannot be resolved.

The more elaborate broadband decoupling techniques involve cycles of composite pulses, which minimise off-resonance effects. The MLEV-4 sequence consists of the motif 90°x180°-x90°x, which is repeated four times. The third and fourth times the phases are inverted. The WALTZ-16 sequence repeats the motif 90°x180°-x270°x sixteen times with systematically varied phase shifts.

Signal enhancement by decoupling

Decoupling is used to increase signal intensities for insensitive or low-abundant nuclei. In addition to the enhancement due to the same total intensity being distributed over less spectral lines the signal is enhanced further by the nuclear Overhauser effect (NOE) during heteronuclear decoupling. If the transition between the α and β states of the more sensitive spin is constantly excited, the levels end up equally populated and thermal equilibrium is disturbed. To regain equilibrium under irradiation the spins will relax through space via dipole-dipole coupling to spins close by. This enhances the spin that is not irradiated up to a factor determined by the gyromagnetic ratio of the irradiated spin divided by twice the gyromagnetic ratio of the spin to be enhanced. For the spin types in Insensitive (emulating 1H and 13C) this is a factor of 2. As soon as decoupling is shut off, the system will start to relax back to thermal equilibrium.
Decoupling sequences are routinely applied to the unobserved nuclei in heteronuclear experiments before the actual pulse sequence, and continued throughout if coupling information is not needed. A disadvantage is the dependence on the distance to and number of neighbouring atoms, making relative intensities in the spectrum arbitrary.
