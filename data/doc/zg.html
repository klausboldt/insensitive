1H-Spectrum

The simplest pulsed NMR experiment is the one-dimensional, homonuclear experiment that is usually performed on protons. (Bruker code: zg)

The pulse sequence consists of a single 90° pulse that flips the spins' z-magnetisation into the x,y-plane and creates observable coherences. The magnetisation is then allowed to freely evolve with time and the FID is recorded.

Set up a two-spin system of I-spins. Set the chemical shifts to Ω1 = 2.2 Hz for spin 1 and Ω2 = -1.7 Hz for spin 2. The coupling constant should be J = 0.25 Hz. To simplify the product operator terms select only spin 1 to be resonant at first in the Settings menu. Note that in a normal homonuclear 1D experiment all spins of the same type are affected by the pulse.

A selective 90°x-pulse turns the I1z operator into -I1y. The free evolution of the transverse magnetisation can be described by the chemical shift operator Ωt1 Iz and results in the terms:

- I1y cos(Ωt1) + I1x sin(Ωt1)

The coupling operator πJ12t1 2I1zI2z evolves the in-phase magnetisation -I1y and I1x into a state of antiphase magnetisation. The product operators then consist of the terms:

- I1y cos(Ωt1) cos(πJ12t1)
+ 2I1xI2z cos(Ωt1) sin(πJ12t1)
+ I1x sin(Ωt1) cos(πJ12t1)
+ 2I1yI2z sin(Ωt1) sin(πJ12t1)

When recording a spectrum run the experiment without, then with strong coupling enabled. Compare the spectra and decrease Ω2 in steps of 1 Hz to see the roofing effect. Use 1024 data points for better resolution.

A similar experiment is the zg30, where the spin vector is only flipped around 30°. The smaller magnetisation in the x,y-plane is compensated by shorter relaxation times which allow for a higher sampling rate. This leads to a higher signal-to-noise ratio and an overall better performance.
