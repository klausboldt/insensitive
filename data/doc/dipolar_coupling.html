Dipolar coupling

Different spins in a molecule do not precess independently. If one spin can have different energy states depending on which state a nearby spin is in, these to spins are coupled. There are two basic mechanisms by which information between coupled spins is exchanged: J-coupling and dipolar coupling.

Dipolar coupling results from each spin generating a magnetic field that is oriented parallel to the nuclear spin vector. Two spins that are close to each other in space experience each other's magnetic field, which leads to an slightly different effective magnetic field Beff at one spin that depends on the orientation of both magnetic dipoles. The strength of the dipole-dipole coupling depends strongly on the spin-spin distance r-3 as well as the gyromagnetic ratios of the coupled spins. It is given by the dipole-dipole coupling constant b12 in units of rad/s.

The orientation dependence is expressed with the secular dipole-dipole coupling constant d12 through the angle Θ between the axis connecting the two spins and the external magnetic field vector B0. The secular approximation is valid in high magnetic fields.

As with the scalar coupling one differentiates between a strong coupling regime where the Larmor frequencies of the coupled spins and therefore the energy levels are similar, and a weak coupling regime with large difference in Larmor frequencies and energy eigenvalues. In the strong coupling regime the coupling depends on all spacial components of the spin. This is the case for a homonuclear spin system.

For heteronuclear spin systems the coupling depends only on the spacial component along the external magnetic field B0.

In an isotropic liquid the molecular orientations are rapidly changed by Brownian motion that is fast on the timescale of NMR. Dipolar couplings are averaged out and cannot be observed. They are therefore not being simulated by Insensitive. The dipolar interactions do play a role in cross relaxation processes and are the mechanism behind the Nuclear Overhauser Enhancement.

In liquid crystals and solids the molecular motion is restricted or suppressed. This leads to a splitting of the NMR signal into two lines for liquid crystals with a coupling constant of 3d12.

In powdered solids the dipole-dipole coupling is the major contribution to spin-spin coupling and leads to the characteristic Pake doublet with the secular coupling constant d between the two maxima.

The shape of the Pake pattern is due to the fact that there are only two possible orientations along the external magnetic field (parallel and antiparallel), but many orientations perpendicular to the B0-field.
