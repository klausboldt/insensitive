Pople Notation

The Pople notation is a way of describing spin systems and coupling patterns with a simple letter code. It is named after its inventor John Pople and is used to distinguish strongly and weakly coupled systems, and magnetically equivalent spins. For weakly coupled spins the chemical shift difference is much larger than the coupling constant, while the two are of the same size for strongly coupled spins. The spins are expressed as letters, whose distance in the latin alphabet symbolised the relative chemical shift. The letters used tend to be limited to A, B, N, M, X, Y.

Strongly coupled systems can lead to complex peak patterns that cannot be analysed manually, but require help by computer simulation. These systems, in which Δν/J (all in Hz) is smaller than 5, are called second order spectra. A larger spectrometer frequency will increase the separation between peaks and thereby simplify the spectrum.

Symbol Coupling pattern
AX Two weakly coupled spins
AB Two strongly coupled spins
A2 Two magnetically equivalent spins
AA' Two spins that are chemically, but not magnetically equivalent
AMX Three weakly coupled spins
ABX Two spins whose peaks are closely spaced and one nucleus with a more distant peak
A2X Two magnetically equivalent spins weakly coupled to a third spin (e.g. a non-diastereotopic CH2 group)
AA'XX' Two weakly coupled pairs of chemically equivalent spins (e.g. 1,1-difluoroethylene)
AA'BB' Two strongly coupled pairs of chemically equivalent spins (e.g. p-substituted aromatic rings)

It is important to note the difference between chemical and magnetical equivalence: chemical equivalence is observed if two or more spins have the same chemical shift, or, in other words, if the spins can change place by a symmetry operation of the molecule. To be magnetically equivalent they must also have the same J coupling to all coupled spins in the system.
In 1,1-difluoroethylene each proton has different coupling constants to the fluorine in cis and trans position. Swapping the protons will also change the coupling to each fluorine nucleus. This leads to a AA'XX' pattern. In 1,1-difluoroallene the coupling constants to both fluorine nuclei is the same, the proton (and fluorine) spins are magnetically equivalent, and the signals appear as a A2X2 pattern.
