Solid state NMR

When measuring powdered solids with NMR one has to consider several effects that average out in solution NMR due to Brownian motion. The main effects in solid state NMR are dipole-dipole coupling, which is the major contribution to the coupling Hamiltonian, chemical shift anisotropies and a much longer T1 and shorter T2 relaxation time compared to solutions.

The strong chemical shift anisotropy results in typical powder patterns, broad signals with sharply defined edges at high and low frequencies. They result from a distribution of different orientations of crystallites in the sample.

Compared with solution NMR the J-coupling plays a small role in solid state NMR compared to the dipole-dipole coupling. Dipolar interactions do not average out in crystalline samples, and as with the chemical shift anisotropy the orientation dependence results in broad line shapes called Pake patterns.

The signal broadening can be removed by spinning the sample at an angle of 54.7° to the axis of the B0-field (Magic Angle Spinning, MAS). At this specific angle the term 3 cos2θ - 1 that describes the orientation dependence of dipolar coupling and chemical shift anisotropy becomes 0 and the signal collapses to a single line. A lower spinning frequency than the width of the powder pattern leads to spinning sidebands, sharp lines that are separated from the isotropic chemical shift by multiples of the spinning frequency.
