Pure Phase 2D Spectra

In 2D spectra the frequency information in the indirect dimension t1 can be coded in two possible ways, as phase or amplitude modulation. The problem with phase modulated spectra as obtained from a single 2D acquisition is that absorption and dispersion line shapes mix after 2D Fourier transformation, resulting in the undesirable phase-twist line shape with broad bases. This cannot be phase corrected. A work-around is to record an amplitude modulated spectrum, in which the real and imaginary part along the indirect dimension evolve purely as cosine and sine, respectively.

States method

The Ruben-States-Haberkorn method realises amplitude modulation by recording two sets of data, one with the original pulse sequence and one where the preparation pulses before t1 are shifted by 90°. After Fourier transformation along t2 the real (or imaginary) parts of each data set are either sine or cosine modulated. Either pair is combined to form a new complex spectrum and Fourier transformed along t1 to yield pure absorption or dispersion line shapes. This method already implements quadrature detection in the indirect dimension, making phase cycling to remove peaks mirrored at the centre frequency obsolete. This can be easily illustrated with Insensitive at the example of a COSY spectrum.

TPPI

The time proportional phase incrementation or Marion-Wüthrich method employs real instead of complex Fourier transformation and introduces frequency discrimination by shifting the preparation pulse phase each time the variable evolution time t1 is increased.

Since a phase shift φ of 90° turns a cosine modulation into a sine modulation, if φ(t1) = ωshiftt1, the whole spectrum is shifted towards higher frequencies. By setting the shift to half the spectral width (90° for every step of t1) one can create a spectrum with no negative frequencies and thus eliminate the mirror peaks without moving the spectrometer frequency from the spectrum's centre. The so treated spectrum is amplitude modulated, but in order to keep the same resolution as the untreated spectrum one must acquire twice the amount of data points by doubling the number of scans, with half the step size (dwell time) in the indirect time domain. This effectively doubles the amount of data and leaves half of the acquired spectrum empty to be discarded. Insensitive automatically adjusts these parameters when selecting TPPI as the detection method.

States-TPPI

Both methods above lead to pure absorption line shapes and emulate quadrature detection in the indirect time domain. Still, strong artefacts appear at the centre of the t1 coordinate, which result from exitation of spins that have relaxed during the variable delay time. To get rid of these two phase cycles can be employed with a phase shift of 180° for all pulses before t1 as well as the receiver. The phase shift reverses only the phase of the unwanted signals, which cancel out between the two runs.

The same results are reached by running an experiment using the States method, but shifting the phases of all pulses before t1 and the receiver by 180° every step of t1, or twice the value employed for TPPI. After complex Fourier transformation only the artefacts are shifted out of the relevant spectral range. No extra phase cycling is needed, but the number of data points has to be doubled to retain spectral resolution.
