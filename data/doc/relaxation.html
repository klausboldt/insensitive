Relaxation

Any spin system that is not in the equilibrium state will relax back to its Boltzmann distribution. This happens via two mechanisms called spin-lattice relaxation and spin-spin relaxation.

The spin-lattice relaxation is an enthalpic process by which the spins exchange energy with their surrounding medium. This can be pictured as a movement of the spins back into the direction of the external magnetic field B0 with the time constant T1. It is therefore also called longitudinal relaxation or T1-relaxation.

The spin-spin relaxation in an entropic process and is characterised by the spins' loss of coherence. The off-diagonal elements of the density matrix and thus all coherences are reduced exponentially with the time constant T2. The spin-spin relaxation is also called transverse relaxation or T2-relaxation.

Relaxation is due to statistical fluctuations in the magnetic field that one spin experiences at a certain time. This can be caused by the rotational movements of molecules and dipole-dipole couplings between closely separated spins. If a spin is in the α-state at time t, it experiences a slightly different field at t + τ, under which the spin state takes the form of a superposition c1α + c2β. The coefficient c2 is the probability for the spin to change state.

The constant τc is called the correlation time. A molecule that rotates fast experiences fast fluctuations and is therefore characterised by a rapidly decaying autocorrelation function and a short τc. A slowly rotating molecule on the other hand has a large correlation time and a slowly decaying autocorrelation function.

The dependance of the transition probability between two spin states is given by the spectral density function. Its normalised form is defined as the Fourier transform of the autocorrelation function.

Short correlation times result in a broad spectral density while long correlation times lead to a narrow spectral density. The transition probability between two states α and β can then be calculated from the local magnetic field squared, the spectral density at the spin's Larmor frequency, the gyromagnetic ratio of the nucleus and the difference of the populations in equilibrium defined by the Boltzmann equation.

The very small Boltzmann factor B is important to consider when describing the individual spins, but can be omitted from the calculations when only the excess ensemble magnetisation is of interest.

The master equation for the longitudinal relaxation returns the difference of the population vector P from thermal equilibrium P0.

For a simple, classical description the transition probability W = -1/T1. For a more accurate treatment of relaxation one has to consider the mechanisms that induce transitions on a microscopic scale. In systems with spins larger than 1/2 the quadrupolar interactions often play the dominant role. In case of only spins 1/2 the possible mechanisms are dipole-dipole couplings, chemical shift anisotropies of tumbling molecules in solution and spin rotation. Dipole-dipole coupling is usually the most important mechanism with CSA becoming more important at large B0-fields.

While through-space dipolar couplings average to zero in isotropic liquids they do cause relaxation by allowing transitions between energy levels. The single quantum transition probability for one spin state is the sum of all transition probabilities to every other spin with which it couples. It depends on the dipolar coupling constant b and the spectral density at the spin's resonance frequency.

The double-quantum transition causes two spins to flip between the αα and the ββ state. It depends on the spectral density at the sum of both spins' resonance frequencies.

The zero-quantum transition causes two spins to flip between the αβ and the βα state. It depends on the spectral density at the at the difference of the two spins' resonance frequencies.

Dipole-dipole couplings are not capable of inducing more than two spin flips per transition. The zero- and double-quantum transitions play a crucial role in the transfer of magnetisation between two spins known as the nuclear Overhauser effect (NOE).
