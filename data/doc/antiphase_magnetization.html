Antiphase magnetisation

Antiphase magnetisation is a state that is created within free evolution of two coupled spins. It represents the coherence of one spin (red vectors) linked to the population of the coupling partner (blue vectors) and is denoted as the product of a transverse magnetisation operator with an operator for z-magnetisation, e.g. 2I1xI2z.

In the vector model of a spin ensemble this equals two magnetisation vectors in opposite directions, which cancel each other out. One vector results from spin 1 bound to spin 2 in α-state and causes the spins to precess faster than an uncoupled spin 1, while the second vector corresponds to spin 1 bound to spin 2 in β-state, resulting in a precession that is slower than an unbound spin 1. This leads to the typical splitting of the coupled spin into a doublet.

Although antiphase magnetisation is not associated with a finite magnetic moment, it is made up of only simple single-quantum coherences and evolves into observable, transverse magnetisation over time.

The name comes from the fact that the peak shape depends on the state of the spin system at the time the acquisition is started. A state of in-phase magnetisation like the coherence -I1y causes a normal doublet, where both peaks have the same sign and phase, while antiphase magnetisation 2I1yI2z results in a characteristic doublet with the two peaks having opposite signs. Antiphase peaks cannot be converted to in-phase peaks by phase correction.
