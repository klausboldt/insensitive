TOCSY

In a COSY spectrum where two chemical shifts cannot be resolved the assignment of two separate spin systems which are not J-coupled to each other becomes difficult. Good examples for this kind of systems are peptides and glycosides where the coupling information is not transferred between monomer units. The TOCSY spectrum shows cross peaks between all spins in a spin network and can be used in these cases to assign complete spin networks.

The pulse sequence is comparable to the COSY sequence, but instead of the second 90°-pulse a spin lock sequence is applied. During this phase the spins are held constant in the rotating frame and the chemical shifts are suppressed. If the Hartmann-Hahn condition is fulfilled and all spins experience the same B1-field the spins are strongly coupled during the spin lock sequence. The then possible flip-flop exchange between the αβ and βα levels causes the magnetisation to flow between all coupled spins.

To fulfil the Hartmann-Hahn condition in the homonuclear TOCSY experiment one needs to apply the same field strength to all coupled spins. This puts high demands on the spectrometer hardware: The wider the spectral range of the pulse should be the higher the pulse power needs to be. A long high power irradiation can heat up the sample and destroy the electronics in the sample head.

Usually pulse sequences instead of continuous wave irradiation are used for the TOCSY experiment. The simplest one is a sequence of closely separated 180°y-pulses. The time between pulses should be short to not allow precession while the total length of the spin lock sequence should be long compared to 1/J.

A more sophisticated sequence is the MLEV-17 sequence, which is constructed from blocks of 90°x-180°y-90°x pulses (R) and the inverted sequence 90°-x-180°-y-90°-x (r) in a supercycle RRrr rRRr rrRR RrrR. The sequence is ended with a 60°x-pulse to invert possible phase errors and repeated to ensure a coherence transfer over the whole spin system.

The effects of the spin lock in the TOCSY experiment is best understood by setting up a linear spin system of three I spins with large differences in chemical shifts and different coupling constants. Disable dipolar relaxation to suppress effects from the ROESY experiment. Apply the 90° pulse to only the first spin. The spin lock is applied by enabling I spin decoupling. Perform the sequence first without and then with strong coupling. Only in the latter case will the magnetisation be transferred to the other spins.
