INEPT

The Insensitive Nuclei Enhanced by Polarisation Transfer experiment is used to transfer magnetisation from a sensitive nucleus (normally 1H) to a less sensitive nucleus via scalar coupling (Bruker code: ineptnd). The maximum efficiency for magnetisation transfer is larger than via NOE during decoupling. The delay has to be optimised for each coupling constant. A complete magnetisation transfer from I to S spins is possible if the delay time is τ = 1/(2J).

Set up a two-spin system consisting of an I-spin and an S-spin with a coupling constant of 0.25 Hz. Set the chemical shifts to ΩI = 0.3 Hz and ΩS = -0.7 Hz, with a distance r = 0.2 nm.

The magnetisation of a certain spin depends on the gyromagnetic ratio of the nucleus. The magnetic moment of 13C is about 1/4 the moment of 1H. The equilibrium state is thus defined by the terms I1z + 0.25 S2z.
A 90°-pulse on the I-spin creates transverse magnetisation. This is followed by a spin echo sandwich with 180°-pulses on both spin types. In this period only the coupling and no chemical shift evolves. Before the second 90°-pulse the spin system is in a state of antiphase magnetisation -2I1xS2z - 0.25 S2z. The 90°y-pulse on the I-spins creates an intermediate state of longitudinal two-spin order 2I1zS2z - 0.25 S2z which is transformed into the antiphase term -2I1zS2y + 0.25 S2y by the 90°x-pulse on the S-spins. Since the magnetisation started on I-spins the amplitude is still that of the sensitive nuclei. The result is an antiphase doublet added to the weaker in-phase doublet of the S-spins. The difference in peak height can be removed by phase cycling.

Because of the antiphase line shapes, decoupling can be performed during the sequence. The antiphase lines would collapse to zero, removing every trace of the gained signal. The INEPT sequence has therefore been extended to let the spins return to in-phase magnetisation with another spin echo sandwich before acquisition (Bruker code: ineptrd). However, this happens on the cost of enhancement due to relaxation during the longer pulse sequence.

The INEPT transfer sequence can be found in many heteronuclear NMR experiments like the HSQC, where it is used to transfer magnetisation both to the insensitive nuclei and back to the more sensitive ones for detection.
