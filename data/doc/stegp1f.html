DOSY

The Diffusion Ordered SpectroscopY is a method to determine diffusion coefficients and hydrodynamic radii in a mixture of molecules using pulsed field gradients.

The pulse sequence of a simple DOSY experiment begins with a 90°x-pulse, which creates transversal magnetisation. This is followed by a pulsed field gradient in z-direction. It dephases the spins during the gradient duration δ, depending on their position in the sample tube and thereby marks them with their position at that time. Afterwards the magnetisation is stored as longitudinal magnetisation by a 90°-x-pulse. This reduced loss of information by T2-relaxation. During a delay time Δ the spins are allowed to diffuse through the sample solution, until the magnetisation is brought back into the transverse plane by another 90°x-pulse. Another gradient pulse refocuses the spins and causes a stimulated spin echo. The FID is acquired when the spin echo reaches its maximum.

If the diffusion time Δ between the beginning of the two gradient pulses is varied systematically, the signal strength reduces according to the diffusion rate kD of the molecules. Fitting the decay of the signal strength to the Stejskal-Tanner expression as a function of Δ results in a pseudo-2D spectrum with the 1H-spectra of the sample mixture separated along the indirect axis.

Here, S is the signal intensity, S0 the signal intensity in the absence of a gradient, D the diffusion coefficient, γ the gyromagnetic ratio and G the gradient strength. The hydrodynamic radius can be calculated from D with the Stokes-Einstein equation.

DOSY Tool Box

Insensitive offers a basic tool set to transform raw 2D decay data into a DOSY spectrum, using a simple method for fitting discrete diffusion coefficients (SPLMOD). The necessary steps need to be performed separately to show the difference to normal 2D NMR processing:

  1. Plot the first trace of the 2D data set at t1 = 0. This data is equal to a normal 1D NMR spectrum.
  2. Identify all peaks in the spectrum by performing peak picking.
  3. Fit the peaks to lorentzian lines to determine their width.
  4. Fit the decay of each peak maximum to a monoexponential function to retrieve the diffusion constant DA and the standard deviation.
  5. Plot each combination of peak position and diffusion constant as a single peak in a 2D spectrum.

The peaks and decay curves are fitted using a Levenberg-Marquardt algorithm. It is important to let the signals decay to zero. A fitting a truncated decay will result in artificially small diffusion constants will large errors.
