Chemical shift

A spin's Larmor frequency does not only depend on the external magnetic field B0 but also on the chemical environment that surrounds the spin. High electron density can shield a nucleus from B0, while electron withdrawing groups deshield a nucleus. The effect is due to circular electron currents that are induced by the external magnetic field and generate a field Binduced.

The induced field can both weaken or strengthen the effective field due to diamagnetic and paramagnetic contributions, respectively. The shift in precession frequencies is small but large enough to be measured. This makes NMR such a useful tool for the determination of chemical structures.

To make the value device independent, chemical shift δ is usually given in ppm relative to the spectrometer frequency as an offset to a standard. In case of 1H and 13C NMR tetramethyl silane (TMS) is used as a standard.

The chemical shift depends on the relative orientation of B0 to the molecule of the measured spin. If one defines a principal coordinate system in which the molecule is rigid, independent from its orientation to B0, the chemical shift tensor δ takes a diagonal form.

The chemical shift in isotropic liquids is averaged to the isotropic chemical shift δ that is equal to the average of the tensor's diagonal elements.

Chemical shift anisotropy

The orientation dependence or chemical shift anisotropy is expressed by the angle Θ between the coordinate of a principal tensor element and B0.

The chemical shift anisotropy plays a role when the spins cannot rotate freely in the magnetic field and the chemical environment varies with the spins' orientation to the B0-field. Strong effects can be observed in aromatic groups, where the magnetic field induces circular currents in the π-system. Spins in plane with the electron current are strongly deshielded and shifted to lower fields, while spins directly on top or below the ring are shifted to higher fields.

In solid state samples this leads to a distribution of chemical shifts that appears as a broad peak shape with steep sides. The principal elements of the chemical shift tensor coincide the low-field and high-field end of the signal (δXX and δYY) and the centre maximum (δZZ).

In axial symmetric, prolate molecules the centre peak coincides with the low field flank of the powder pattern. In axial symmetric, oblate molecules the centre peak coincides with the high field edge.
