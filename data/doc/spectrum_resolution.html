Spectrum resolution

There are two parameters that influence the way an acquired spectrum turns out. They are the dwell time and the number of data points. Both can be set in the acquisition settings of Insensitive's main window.

The dwell time or sampling interval τsample is the time delay between two data points. It determines the high frequency limit of the spectral resolution, called the Nyquist frequency. If τsample is smaller than 1/(2ω0) with ω0 being the oscillation's angular frequency, the signal will oscillate several times between two adjacent data points and not be recorded. The signal is therefore mistaken for a lower frequency, the spectrum wraps around and the peak appears at a wrong position. A longer dwell time translates to a smaller frequency window being visible in the spectrum.

With a constant dwell time the total number of data points N that is recorded determines the spectrum resolution. To resolve peaks in more detail, N must be raised. This can be done either by actually acquiring more data, or by simply appending a string of zeros at the end of the FID, called zero filling. While this method does not increase the information encoded in the spectrum, i.e. it does not resolve more peaks than in the original data, it does smoothen the spectrum. Due to the algorithm of Fast Fourier Transformation (FFT) the number of data points must be a power of two.

The lowest frequency resolved in the spectrum is determined by the total detection time Nτsample.

In addition, two factors that contribute to spectral resolution and cannot simply be adjusted at the spectrometer are spin relaxation, which broadens the peaks, and the magnetic field strength. At higher fields the energy levels are more separated and the resolution of individual peaks is increased.
