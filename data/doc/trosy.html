TROSY

In a heteronuclear spin system of an I and an S spin, e.g. a coupled spin pair of a 1H and a 15N nucleus in a protein backbone, the S spin is subject to dipolar coupling and chemical shift anisotropy. The magnetic fields due to the proton spin orientation and the electron ring currents are always opposed if the proton is in its β state and point in the same direction if it is in its α state, independent from the molecule's orientation to the external magnetic field. Dipolar relaxation of the hetero spin is therefore dependent on the state of the coupling partner and causes a doublet with significantly different line widths. The peak caused by coupling to a proton in β state is narrowed, while the linewidth for the spin coupled to a proton in α state is increased. This effect is more pronounced at high magnetic fields.

The transverse relaxation optimised spectroscopy (TROSY) removed the effects of line broadening in the spectrum by acquiring the FID without decoupling and then designing the pulse sequence in a way that the magnetisation is stored in slowly decaying coherences. If decoupling was applied, the line width would be averaged and caused undecipherable spectra even if half of the coherences gave sharp peaks. The family of TROSY sequences is the basis for measuring large proteins with very long correlation times τc.

TROSY spectra start from a state of longitudinal two-spin order ±2IzSz, which is created by a 90°x pulse on the I spins, followed by a spin echo sandwich on both spins with a total duration of 1/(2JIS), and a second 90° pulse with a ±90° phase shift. The TROSY sequence can be included in experiments like the HSQC (minus the decoupling pulses), where the broad signals are then removed by phase cycling.
